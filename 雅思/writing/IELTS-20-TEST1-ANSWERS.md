Overall, the total population of New York City increased dramatically over the period. Although Manhattan contained the majority of the city's residents in 1800, its share declined considerably, while the other four districts became home to the great majority of the population by 2000.

New York City's total population rose from 79,216 in 1800 to 3,437,202 in 1900, before reaching 8,009,185 in 2000. Manhattan also experienced substantial growth during the first century, with its population increasing from 60,515 to 1,850,093. However, this figure then fell slightly to 1,538,096 in 2000. More significantly, Manhattan's share of the total population dropped from 76% to 54%, and finally to only 19%.

By contrast, the combined population of Brooklyn, the Bronx, Queens and Staten Island increased continuously, from 18,701 in 1800 to 1,587,109 in 1900 and 6,471,089 in 2000. Their proportion of the city's population therefore rose from 24% to 46%, before reaching 81%. In 1900, the combined population of these districts was still slightly lower than that of Manhattan, but by 2000 it was more than four times as large.




# TASK 2
Access to clean water is undoubtedly essential for human health and dignity. However, I completely disagree that every household should receive its water supply free of charge. Water provision involves substantial and continuing costs, and charging consumers is necessary both to fund the system and to discourage unnecessary consumption.

The first reason is that clean water is not free to produce or deliver. Governments and water companies must pay for water treatment, pumping, quality testing, pipe maintenance and the replacement of ageing infrastructure. These expenses continue long after a water network has been installed. If households paid nothing, the entire cost would have to be covered by taxpayers. Since public budgets are limited, spending more on free household water would leave less money available for hospitals, schools, transport and other essential services. In other words, removing water charges would not eliminate the cost; it would merely transfer it to the public.

A second problem is that free water could encourage waste and create an unfair system. When consumers pay according to the amount they use, they have a financial reason to repair leaking taps and avoid excessive consumption. By contrast, if water were free, heavy users could consume far more without facing any additional cost. A wealthy household with a large garden or swimming pool might therefore receive a much larger public subsidy than a low-income family. Rather than making water free for everyone, governments should charge all households for their actual consumption and provide direct financial assistance to people who genuinely cannot afford their bills. Cash support would allow poorer families to meet their essential needs while preserving the incentive to use water responsibly.

In conclusion, access to clean water should be guaranteed, but this does not mean that household water should have a price of zero. A fairer and more sustainable policy is to charge consumers for what they use while offering targeted financial support to those in need.
